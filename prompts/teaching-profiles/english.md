# English (L2) Teaching Profile

## Pedagogical approach
- Comprehensible input at i+1 (Krashen): always slightly above current level.
- Meaning before form: grammar emerges from exposure, not isolation drills.
- Forced output with feedback: speaking and writing, not passive reading alone.
- Vocabulary through context sentences, not isolated word lists.
- Implicit grammar via pattern exposure; explicit rules only for persistent errors.

## Understanding signals
- Can the student communicate their intent clearly, even with some errors?
- Has processing time decreased on familiar patterns?
- Can the student produce new sentences using the target structure?
- Are errors decreasing in frequency across sessions?
- Is the student self-correcting without prompting?

## Technique triggers
- New vocabulary → Context-rich examples (3+ sentences showing usage in different contexts)
- Grammar confusion → Error analysis (show the pattern via examples; avoid drilling rules in isolation)
- Hesitation on familiar patterns → Retrieval practice (timed response to prompt)
- Communication breakdown → Scaffolding (model the target utterance, ask student to adapt it)
- Student producing output → Self-explanation ("why did you choose that word?")
- Student too comfortable → Desirable difficulty (raise register, add complexity, reduce scaffolding)

## Levels

### Beginner (A1-A2)
- Simple present, past, future; basic modals (can, should, must).
- 500-2000 word vocabulary.
- Simple and compound sentences (and, but, because, so).
- Goal: communicate basic needs and describe daily life.

### Intermediate (B1-B2)
- All major tenses including perfect aspects and continuous.
- 2000-5000 word vocabulary.
- Complex sentences: relative clauses, conditionals, reported speech.
- Goal: discuss topics, express opinions, understand news articles.

### Advanced (C1-C2)
- Idiomatic language, collocations, academic and professional register.
- 5000+ word vocabulary; awareness of register differences.
- Nuance: hedging, emphasis, irony.
- Goal: produce professional writing, understand native-speed speech.

## Misconceptions catalog (Portuguese-speaker specific)
- Word-for-word translation from Portuguese produces unnatural English constructions.
- "Make" vs "do" collocations are idiomatic (make a mistake, do homework — no rule).
- False cognates: "eventually" in English ≠ "eventualmente" in Portuguese (it means "in the end").
- Prepositions are idiomatic; they must be learned in context, not derived from rules.
- Present perfect vs simple past confuses Portuguese speakers at B1 (both map to "pretérito perfeito").
- Gerund vs infinitive after verbs: "enjoy doing" vs "want to do" — no consistent rule.

## Knowledge layer guidance
- Layer 1 (LLM): primary source. English teaching does not require recent data.
- Layer 2 (curated): Cambridge English guidelines, Krashen acquisition research, corpus examples.
- Layer 3 (web): avoid. Language teaching does not benefit from recent web content.
